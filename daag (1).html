Submission Standards
Only submit publicly visible comments from public posts or pages. Do not submit content from private groups, direct messages, or closed conversations. Fabricated or manipulated screenshots will result in a permanent submission restriction.
Contributor Guidelines
What to submit: Publicly visible comments from public Facebook posts or pages that contain misogynistic, victim-blaming, threatening, or dehumanizing language related to incidents of gender violence in Bangladesh.
What NOT to submit: Private messages, content from closed groups, comments unrelated to gender violence, personal disputes, political disagreements that do not involve gender-based abuse, or any content you have fabricated or manipulated.
Screenshot requirements: Screenshots must be unedited, uncropped, and unannotated. They must clearly show the comment text, the commenter's profile name, and the post context. Timestamps should be visible where possible. Do not redact or highlight any portion.
Accuracy expectations: Select the most accurate abuse category and incident type. If unsure, err on the side of caution and add a contextual note explaining your uncertainty. Moderators will reclassify if needed.
Submitter responsibility: By submitting, you confirm the evidence is genuine and unmanipulated. False submissions waste moderator time and undermine the archive's credibility. Repeated false submissions will result in permanent restriction.

Methodology & Ethics
How Daag Works

Our standards, principles, and accountability framework. We hold ourselves to the same standards of transparency and evidence that we demand of the archive.

Guiding Principles

Daag operates as a civic accountability initiative, not a harm platform. Every editorial, design, and architectural decision is filtered through this distinction. The archive exists to make harmful online discourse visible — to researchers, advocates, journalists, and policymakers — not to facilitate retaliation or public shaming.

We believe accountability and dignity are not in conflict. It is possible to document harmful behaviour rigorously while preserving the humanity of all parties involved.

Principle 01
Evidence Over Outrage
Every submission is evaluated on verifiable evidence. Context, source URL, and screenshot authenticity are assessed before any entry is published.
Principle 02
No Private Data
We document only publicly visible comments from public posts. Private messages, closed groups, and direct messages are outside our scope and will be rejected.
Principle 03
Moderated Before Published
Nothing enters the public archive without editorial review. Automated intake is only for queue management, not for publication decisions.
Principle 04
Patterns, Not People
Our analytics and reporting focus on behavioural patterns and category trends. We do not publish leaderboards of individuals or rank entries by volume of comments from a single source.
Principle 05
Appeals Are Binding
Successful appeals result in removal or reclassification. Appeals are reviewed by a separate panel from the original moderator. All decisions are documented and auditable.
AI Classification Limitations

We use AI-assisted toxicity scoring as a supporting tool for human moderators, not as a decision-making engine. All AI outputs carry an explicit confidence rating and are clearly flagged as advisory.

AI models may struggle with Bangla slang, regional idioms, coded language, and culturally specific victim-blaming rhetoric that does not match training data patterns. Human review is mandatory for all borderline cases and all entries in Bangla or Banglish.

Required Disclosure
All archive entries that received AI classification assistance display this notice: "AI analysis was used to support — not determine — this categorization. Final decision made by a human moderator." This disclosure cannot be removed by moderators.
Removal Requests

Any individual whose comment appears in the archive may submit a formal removal request. Grounds for removal include: submission in bad faith, manipulated screenshot, missing critical context that materially changes meaning, or demonstrable rehabilitation.

We do not remove entries solely because the subject finds them embarrassing or disagrees with the categorisation. The archive's integrity depends on consistency. However, we reassess every request in good faith within 21 working days and document all decisions.

Moderator Standards

Daag's moderation team is the backbone of the archive's credibility. All moderators are held to rigorous standards of language competence, training, and conduct. We invest in their welfare and continuously evaluate their work.

Standard 01
Language Proficiency
All moderators must demonstrate fluency in Bangla and working proficiency in English. Moderators handling Banglish content must pass additional assessment on regional slang, coded language, and context-dependent idioms common in Bangladeshi Facebook discourse.
Standard 02
Training Programme
New moderators complete a mandatory 20-hour training programme covering: evidence verification techniques, category definitions, severity assessment, cultural context for gender-based violence in Bangladesh, trauma-informed review practices, and platform-specific manipulation detection.
Standard 03
Operational Process
Every moderator decision is logged with rationale. Category assignments require written justification. Severity ratings follow a standardised rubric. Borderline cases are escalated to senior review. No moderator reviews more than 30 submissions per session to maintain quality.
Standard 04
Wellbeing Protocol
Moderators are exposed to distressing content daily. Daag provides mandatory rest intervals, session time limits, access to peer support groups, and quarterly mental health check-ins. Moderators may step back at any time without penalty.
Standard 05
Code of Conduct
Moderators must not use the archive for personal purposes, discuss pending submissions outside the platform, or take actions based on personal opinion rather than evidence. Violations result in immediate suspension and review by the oversight committee.
Anti-Harassment Policy

Daag will permanently revoke submission access and report to relevant authorities any user who submits false evidence, uses this platform to target individuals for reasons unrelated to gender violence discourse, or coordinates use of this archive as part of a harassment campaign.

Our moderation team actively monitors for patterns of abuse. If you believe someone is misusing this platform, contact us directly with evidence.

Enforcement Mechanisms
Mechanism 01
Submission Fingerprinting
All submissions are fingerprinted using metadata, timing patterns, and browser characteristics. Repeated bad-faith submissions from the same source are flagged automatically for moderator review and may trigger immediate restriction.
Mechanism 02
Repeat Offender Tracking
Submitters who accumulate multiple rejected or flagged submissions are tracked internally. Escalation tiers include: warning, temporary suspension, permanent restriction, and referral to legal authorities where applicable.
Mechanism 03
Archive Redistribution Monitoring
We monitor for coordinated redistribution of archive content for harassment purposes across social media platforms. Detected campaigns are documented and may be reported to the relevant platforms and authorities.
Mechanism 04
Referral to Authorities
In cases of confirmed malicious use — including fabrication of evidence, coordinated targeting, or weaponisation of the archive — Daag reserves the right to refer all documented evidence to Bangladesh Cyber Tribunal or relevant law enforcement bodies.
Mechanism 05
Community Reporting
Any user may report suspected misuse of the platform via a dedicated reporting channel. All reports are reviewed within 7 working days. Reporters are protected under our confidentiality policy and will not be identified to the reported party.
Legal Compliance

Daag operates with awareness of the legal landscape in Bangladesh, including the Digital Security Act (DSA) 2018, the Cyber Security Act 2023, and relevant provisions of the Information and Communication Technology Act. We do not claim legal immunity, but we take proactive steps to ensure our documentation practices remain defensible.

Compliance 01
Digital Security Act Awareness
All archived content is drawn from publicly visible posts. We do not intercept private communications, hack accounts, or access restricted content. Our documentation practices are designed to fall within the scope of public interest journalism and civic accountability.
Compliance 02
Defamation Safeguards
The archive documents publicly made statements with verifiable screenshots and source URLs. We do not editorialize, add opinion, or characterize individuals beyond what is evidenced. All entries carry clear context and attribution to the original public post.
Compliance 03
Right to Reply
Every archived individual has access to a formal appeals process. The right to contest, contextualise, or request removal is built into the platform's core architecture and is accessible without legal representation.
Compliance 04
Cooperation with Authorities
Daag will comply with lawful orders from Bangladeshi courts or regulatory bodies. If compelled by law, we will provide relevant records while safeguarding submitter confidentiality to the extent legally permitted.
Data Retention Policy

All data held by Daag is subject to defined retention schedules. We do not retain data indefinitely without justification, and we distinguish between published archive data and internal operational data.

Retention 01
Published Archive Entries
Verified and published entries remain in the archive indefinitely unless removed through a successful appeal. This permanence is intentional — the archive's value depends on its reliability as a long-term record.
Retention 02
Rejected Submissions
Submissions that are rejected during moderation are retained internally for 90 days to support pattern-detection and anti-abuse monitoring, then permanently deleted including all associated screenshots.
Retention 03
Appeal Records
All appeal submissions and decisions are retained for 2 years following resolution to provide an audit trail. After the retention period, records are anonymised and retained in aggregate form only.
Retention 04
Submitter Contact Information
Optional submitter contact details are retained for 180 days following submission to allow moderation follow-up. After this period, contact information is permanently deleted. It is never published or shared externally.
Screenshot Handling

Screenshots are central to the archive's evidentiary function. We treat them as sensitive documents and apply strict handling procedures at every stage — from submission through storage to public display.

Screenshot 01
Verification Process
Every screenshot is verified against the source URL and post context before publication. Moderators check for manipulation signs including inconsistent fonts, pixel artifacts, timestamp anomalies, and missing UI elements.
Screenshot 02
Secure Storage
Screenshots are stored in encrypted, access-controlled storage. Only authenticated moderators and system administrators can access raw screenshot files. Public-facing screenshots are served through a restricted display layer with blur applied by default.
Screenshot 03
Metadata Stripping
All uploaded screenshots are stripped of EXIF data and other embedded metadata before storage. This removes GPS coordinates, device identifiers, and other information that could compromise submitter privacy.
Screenshot 04
Public Display Controls
Screenshots in the public archive are blurred by default and require explicit user action to reveal. This prevents incidental exposure during browsing and gives viewers control over when they see potentially distressing content.

About
Why Daag Exists

In Bangla, দাগ means stain — the kind that doesn't wash off. The name is deliberate. When men post victim-blaming, misogynistic, or violent commentary on public Facebook posts about gender violence, that behaviour should leave a record. This project was built in response to a pattern: when incidents of rape, acid violence, or domestic abuse occur in Bangladesh, the comment sections fill with cruelty — and then disappear. Daag exists to make sure they don't.

The Problem
Online cruelty
after violence

When news of a rape, acid attack, or domestic violence case spreads on Facebook, the comment sections often become a second site of violence. Victims are blamed for their clothing, behaviour, family background, and character. Perpetrators are defended. Survivors are silenced.

These comments disappear quickly — moderated, deleted, or buried by newer content. But while they exist, they shape public discourse, discourage survivors from speaking, and signal to perpetrators that their community supports them.

Daag exists to preserve a systematic record of this discourse — not for outrage, but for understanding. For researchers, journalists, advocates, and policymakers who need evidence to make the case for change.
