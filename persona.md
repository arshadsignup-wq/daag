> 12 user personas representing the full spectrum of people who interact with the Daag platform — from evidence submitters and searchers to moderators, researchers, and bad actors.

## 1. Evidence Submitter (Survivor's Friend)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Name** | Fatima Akter |
| **Age** | 26 |
| **Location** | Dhaka, Bangladesh |
| **Occupation** | Private university lecturer |
| **Digital literacy** | Moderate — comfortable with Facebook, WhatsApp, basic web browsing |

### Goals
- Document the victim-blaming comments her friend received after reporting a sexual assault
- Ensure the comments are preserved even if the perpetrators delete them
- Contribute to a record that might help others identify these individuals in the future

### Frustrations
- Watched her friend's rape case comment section fill with cruelty — then saw the comments disappear within days
- Feels helpless that there is no consequence for public abuse
- Worries that submitting evidence might expose her identity or put her at risk

### Scenario
Fatima's colleague was raped. A widely-shared Facebook post about the case attracted dozens of victim-blaming comments. One commenter wrote "she went out at midnight, what did she expect?" and received 200 reactions. Fatima screenshots the comment, the commenter's profile, and the source post.

### Platform Interaction
1. Visits the Evidence Submission Portal
2. Enters the perpetrator's Facebook profile URL and the source post URL
3. Uploads the unedited screenshot
4. Selects "Victim Blaming" as the category and "Rape / Sexual Violence" as the incident type
5. Adds a contextual note explaining the incident and the coordinated nature of the comments
6. Optionally provides her email in the Submitter Reference section (relationship: "Friend or Family of Survivor")
7. Submits and receives a reference number

| **Age** | 29 |
| **Location** | Rajshahi, Bangladesh |
| **Occupation** | Sociology graduate student |
| **Digital literacy** | Very high — research skills, data analysis, content moderation training |

### Goals
- Review pending submissions accurately and consistently
- Ensure no fabricated or manipulated evidence enters the public archive
- Maintain the archive's credibility and protect both survivors and subjects
- Contribute to a project that aligns with her academic focus on gender-based violence

### Frustrations

- Some submissions are borderline — the line between legitimate accountability and personal vendetta is not always clear

### Scenario
Sadia logs into the Moderation Console and sees 47 pending submissions. She reviews each one: checking the screenshot against the source URL, verifying the Facebook profile, assessing the category assignment, and reading the AI toxicity score (advisory only).

### Platform Interaction
1. Opens the Moderation Console
2. Reviews the queue — sorted by submission time
3. For each item: checks screenshot authenticity, verifies URLs, reads contextual notes
4. Reviews the AI confidence score (treats it as advisory)
5. Takes action: Approve (publish to archive), Reject (remove from queue), or Hold (escalate)
6. Logs her rationale for each decision
7. Takes a mandatory break after 30 submissions

## 10. Bad Actor (Weaponisation Attempt)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Name** | Anonymous / Pseudonymous |
| **Age** | Unknown |
| **Location** | Unknown |
| **Occupation** | Unknown |
| **Digital literacy** | Variable — ranges from low (impulsive) to high (coordinated) |

### Goals
- Submit fabricated evidence against a personal enemy, ex-partner, business rival, or political opponent
- Use the archive as a weapon in a personal dispute unrelated to gender violence
- Coordinate a group to mass-submit false evidence against a target
- Scrape the archive to build a harassment list

### Frustrations
- The moderation process catches fabricated screenshots
- Rate limiting prevents mass submissions
- Honeypot fields detect automated bot submissions
- Submission fingerprinting flags repeated bad-faith attempts

### Scenario
A man wants to damage his ex-wife's new partner's reputation. He fabricates a screenshot showing a victim-blaming comment, uses a different browser to submit it, and hopes the moderation team won't check carefully.

### Platform Interaction (Attempted)
1. Creates a fabricated screenshot using image editing software
2. Submits it through the Evidence Submission Portal
3. The submission enters the moderation queue
4. A moderator checks the screenshot against the source URL — the URL shows no such comment
5. The submission is rejected as fabricated
6. The submitter's fingerprint is flagged for repeat offender tracking
7. If a pattern is detected, submission access is permanently revoked and evidence is referred to authorities

### How Daag Defends Against This
- Mandatory two-URL verification (profile + source post)
- Screenshot authenticity checking by trained moderators
- Rate limiting (30-second cooldown, 10 submissions per session)
- Honeypot fields to catch bots
- Submission fingerprinting and repeat offender tracking
- Permanent restriction and referral to authorities for confirmed fabrication
